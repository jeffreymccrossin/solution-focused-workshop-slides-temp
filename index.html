Coaching vs Therapy: Scope and Safety

  • Coaching supports goals, action, and collaboration.
  • Therapy identifies and treats diagnosable mental health conditions.

Coaching - Focuses on setting and achieving specific goals. - Oriented to problem-solving and future strategies. - Does not diagnose or treat mental health disorders.

Therapy - Addresses mental health issues and emotional distress. - Often involves diagnosis and treatment of mental health conditions. - Focuses on healing, understanding the past, and emotional processing.

Coaching vs Therapy: Examples

  • Severe anxiety or depression
  • Unresolved trauma themes
  • Substance misuse concerns
  • Risk of harm (self or others)
  • Outside coaching scope
  • Stay in scope: document and refer
  • Client expresses severe anxiety, depression, or other mental health concerns.
  • Discussion of trauma or past experiences seems unresolved and is impacting current well-being.
  • Signs of substance misuse or other harmful behaviors.
  • Client mentions or exhibits signs of harm to self or others.
  • Client’s challenges are beyond coaching scope (for example, serious relationship conflict or grief).
  • Stay in scope, document clearly, and refer when needed.

Section 2: Solution-Focused Coaching (SFC): Background, Stance and Assumptions

Background: What Is Solution-Focused Coaching?

  • A collaborative, goal-oriented conversation approach
  • Focuses on direction, strengths, and workable steps
  • Designed for real-world constraints

Background: Brief Origins

  • Emerged from brief, practice-based therapy traditions
  • Controversial: Years of therapy, detailed history not required
  • Adapted across health, education, social, and community settings
  • Core thread: useful conversations that move people forward

Stance: SFC - A Way of Thinking

  • SFC is a stance before it is a technique set.
  • Focus on preferred futures, resources, and movement.
  • Keep language practical, accessible, and aligned with the family.

Stance: Moving Toward Solutions

  • We’re less interested in pushing certain kinds of solutions onto families (we’re not the experts of their lives).
  • We’re interested in uncovering pathways that allow families to discover solutions.
  • Family beliefs about the problem may get in the way of seeing solutions.

Stance: Leading from Behind

  • Families know themselves the best, though their views can be temporarily distorted.
  • We can help them to
    • get back in touch with existing resources and identify new ones,
    • work toward shifting beliefs about the problem,
    • and co-develop preferred outcomes.

Stance: How We Talk About Our Work

  • We’re as interested in the family and caregivers as we are the individual living with the disability or illness.
  • Expanding our focus beyond the individual gives us more opportunities to intervene.
  • A systemic approach: when something happens to one part of the family, other members need to adjust and adapt.

Assumptions

Assumptions of SFC

9 key assumptions guide conversations that:

Focus on what people want instead, not on fixing what’s wrong.

Pay attention to what already works and when problems are less present.

Assume clients have skills, resources, and partial solutions.

Support small, meaningful steps rather than big fixes.

Use different kinds of conversations to build solutions.

Assumption 1: Focus on Solutions

  • We don’t need to fully understand or fix the problem to move forward
  • Attention shifts toward what would make life better
  • This reduces blame and overwhelm

Fixing assumes something is broken.
Solutions assume something is possible.

We’re not ignoring problems — we’re choosing where attention goes.

Assumption 2: Look Forward

  • Conversations are oriented toward what clients want next
  • The past matters, but it doesn’t have to lead
  • Preferred futures create direction and hope

Ask yourself: what are they hoping will be different?

This is about direction, not denial of history.

Assumption 3: Do More of What Works

  • Clients are already doing some things that help
  • Even small successes matter
  • Change builds on what is already happening

This is about amplification, not invention.

Often people are already halfway there — they just don’t name it that way.

Assumption 4: Problems Are Not Constant

  • Difficulties come and go
  • There are moments when the problem is less intense or absent
  • These moments contain useful information

When is this not happening — or happening less?

Those moments tell us more than long problem descriptions.

Assumption 5: Building on Existing Skills

  • Clients already have skills and coping strategies
  • Coaching helps make these visible and usable
  • New possibilities often emerge from familiar strengths

We’re uncovering skills, not installing them.

Stress hides competence — it doesn’t erase it.

Assumption 6: Clients Have Solutions

  • Solutions are often partial, unclear, or blocked
  • Clients are experts in their own lives
  • Our role is to help enable what they already know

Expertise doesn’t disappear under pressure — it gets harder to access.

Our job is to help bring it back into view.

Assumption 7: Small Steps Matter

  • Change does not need to be dramatic to be meaningful
  • Small shifts can lead to larger change over time
  • Progress is often incremental

“What would count as a little better?”

Momentum matters more than magnitude.

Assumption 8: Solutions Don’t Have to Match the Problem

  • Helpful changes may seem indirect
  • What works does not always look logical from the outside
  • Fit matters more than explanation

If it helps, it helps — even if we don’t fully understand why.

We privilege usefulness over elegance.

Assumption 9: Different Conversations Create Change

  • Talking about problems and talking about solutions are different skills
  • Language shapes what people notice and imagine
  • Coaching focuses on conversations that open possibilities

This is about how we talk, not just what we ask.

Different conversations invite different futures.

Case Example Revisited

  • Mother of 9-year-old with developmental needs calls support hotline.
  • Feels done; doubts support will help; prior services felt unhelpful.
  • Flat tone, brief answers, declines group support.
  • Time pressure: night shift and no backup childcare.

In team debrief, one responder says: “Honestly, she sounds unmotivated and resistant. We should just send resources by email and close unless she calls back.”

Next Steps: How can we apply SFC assumptions to this case?

  • Clients have resources: Treat “flat tone/brief answers” as fatigue and protection, not resistance.
  • Collaboration over expert-driven fixing: Ask what she wants from this contact before offering options.
  • Small changes matter: Co-create one realistic next step that fits night-shift/no-childcare constraints.
  • Exceptions exist: Explore when caregiving felt even slightly more manageable and what was different.
  • Future focus: Define a near-future sign of progress (next 48 hours or this week).
  • Do more of what works: Identify any prior support element that helped, even briefly, and adapt it.
  • Language shapes change: Replace “unmotivated/resistant” with context-based descriptions and strengths.
  • Pacing and fit: Keep contact brief, concrete, and choice-based; do not overload with resources.

Section 3: SFC Skills (Part 1)

  • Build fluency with core SFC questioning patterns.
  • Use examples to translate assumptions into usable language.

Key Approaches to SFC

  • Solution talk & future focus: People can get stuck trying to understand how the problem developed.
  • Exceptions: Problems do not happen all the time, nor at the same intensity.
  • Coping: How do they manage?
  • Scaling questions: Take stock of where clients have been, where they are now, and what they expect next.
  • Give compliments with specific evidence.
  • Summarize, validate, and empathize to join with the client and family.

Bridge: Assumptions -> Skills

  • Assumptions set direction.
  • Skills operationalize assumptions in real conversations.
  • Technique quality depends on joining, pacing, and fit.

Solution Talk: What Works?

  • Explore past attempts (failed and successful) to solve the problem.
  • What worked before, even just a little?
  • Exceptions to the problem: when is the problem absent, or less intense?
  • What works for some family members in certain situations?
  • This approach contrasts common red-flag words from clients: everyone, always, never.

What Works (Exceptions)? Example (1/4)

Coach: Emma, what would you like to see happen from our time together?
Caregiver: I want more balance. I’m overwhelmed and don’t know where to turn.

What Works? Example (2/4)

Coach: Can you recall moments, even briefly, when things felt more manageable?
Caregiver: Home care support helped, but it was hard to access.

What Works? Example (3/4)

Coach: What difference did those few hours make for you?
Caregiver: I felt less stressed and more like myself.

What Works? Example (4/4)

Coach: If you had more moments like those, what might that look like?
Caregiver: I guess I’d have more hours of home support but I’ll have to fight for them… I mean, even if my brother came over once a week that would give me a break.

What to Do with Problem Talk (1/3)

  • Why do people seem to repeat themselves over and over?
  • How can we manage this in a gentle way and move forward?
  • Core reason people repeat problem talk: they do not feel understood.

What to Do with Problem Talk (2/3)

  • Create just enough space for the family to talk about the problem so they feel heard and understood.
  • Then make space for a new way of talking about the problem.
  • Ask what is happening around the problem.

What to Do with Problem Talk - Example (3/3)

Client: [talks in circles about the problem]
Coach (validate + pivot): I can really hear how much of a challenge this is. Can we try a different angle?
Follow-up:
Option A (exception): “Can you tell me about moments when this problem does not show up?”
Option B (intensity): “When it does show up, when is it less intense?”

  • Since school restarted, 4:00-7:00 PM is chaotic most days.
  • “It’s always chaos. I’ve tried everything. Nothing works.”

Instructions

  • Triads: Coach, Caregiver, Observer.

  • Coach: name 1 exception + 1 next step.
  • Observer: capture shifts from “always/never” to specifics.
  • Debrief (4-5 min): best question, exception, next step.

Question Bank (Coach)

  • “When was this even 10% less intense?”
  • “What was different in that moment?”
  • “Who was doing what that helped, even a little?”
  • “What would tell you this evening is one point better?”
  • “What small part of that could you repeat this week?”

Coping Questions

  • Used when things feel stuck, overwhelming, or crisis-oriented
  • Acknowledge difficulty without reinforcing the problem story
  • Highlight how people are already managing to get through
  • Restore a sense of competence, dignity, and agency
  • Create enough stability to move toward exceptions and next steps
  • Coping questions are not about minimizing suffering.
  • They recognize what it takes to keep going when things are hard.
  • They pause the problem spiral and help clients notice resilience already present, even when it does not feel like “strength.”

Coping Questions - Example (1/3)

Coach: I understand caregiving can be incredibly challenging. When it feels overwhelming, how do you usually cope?
Caregiver: Sometimes I take a short walk, or I call a friend. It doesn’t always help, but it gives me a little break.

Coping Questions - Example (2/3)

Coach: Those sound like valuable strategies. How effective are they in reducing your stress?
Caregiver: They help a bit, but I often feel guilty for taking time for myself.
Coach: What if you could take these breaks without feeling guilty? What might that look like?

Coping Questions - Example (3/3)

Caregiver: I guess reminding myself that taking care of my needs helps me be a better caregiver.
Coach: That is a great perspective. How might you incorporate these breaks more regularly so they become a guilt-free part of caregiving?

What’s Better?

  • At each meeting, explore how things have improved.

What’s Better? Initial Meeting

  • “Sometimes people tell me that while they were waiting for service, or after they finally booked the appointment, that something changed. Something got a little better.”

  • “Can you tell me what changed for you and your family? Even just a little?”

What’s Better? Follow-Up Meetings

  • “Sometimes people tell me that after we meet, there starts to be some changes in their lives. Even if not related to our meeting, can you tell me about what’s better? Even just a little?”

What’s Better? Capitalizing on Responses

  • [Better] Wow, it seems like things are going in the right direction. What do you think allowed that to happen?
  • [Same] Ok, so things haven’t gotten worse. How did you and your family manage to keep things steady?
  • [Worse] Sorry to hear things seem to be tough right now (summarize, empathy & validation)

Scaling Questions

  • Creates a shared, concrete snapshot of where things are now.
  • Good for testing your understanding.
  • It helps surface existing progress and resources (“why not lower?”).
  • It supports collaborative goal setting by identifying the next small gain (“one point higher”).

Scaling Questions - Example (A 1/3)

Coach: On a scale of 1-10, where 1 is the worst and 10 is much easier to manage, where are you today?
Client: Maybe a 5.5.
Coach (Option A): Why 5.5 and not 5?
Coach (Option B): Why 5.5 and not 0?

Scaling Questions - Example (A 2/3)

Coach (option A): What would be happening in your life when you notice you are at a solid 6?
Coach (option B): What will you notice when you find yourself at a 10?

Scaling Questions - Example (A 3/3)

Coach (option C): At what number would you be satisfied? What would be happening then?
Coach (relational): What would others notice about you?
Coach (relational): What number might your partner/mother/child guess you are at today?

Scaling Questions - Example (B 1/2)

Coach: So, you’d be satisfied with an 8. What would be happening then?
Client: My husband would be drinking less.

Scaling Questions - Example (B 2/2)

Coach: Right, if he were drinking less you’d notice a big difference. What difference would that make to you if he were to be sober?

Coach focus: Acknowledge and redirect to client meaning, and shift from the absence of drinking to the presence of sobriety.

Scaling Questions - Example (C 1/4)

Coach: Who would be the first to notice that you’re at an 8?
Client: Probably my son.

Scaling Questions - Example (C 2/4)

Coach: And what would he notice?
Client: He’d see me smiling more.

Scaling Questions - Example (C 3/4)

Coach (option A): Smiling more [repeats client’s words for emphasis].
Option A [turns to son]: Do you think she’s right? Would you be the first to notice?
Option A [follow-up]: What do you think you might notice in her when she’s at an 8?
Option A [follow-up]: When she’s at an 8, what number might you find yourself at?

Scaling Questions - Example (C 4/4)

Coach (option B): Smiling more [repeats client’s words for emphasis].
Coach [to client]: And when he notices you smiling more, what difference will that make to you?
Client: That would mean everything. I’d feel like a better mom. Less guilty.

  • What do you imagine some challenges are with SFC?
  • Invite participants to name common myths or concerns they have about SFC.

Dispelling Common Myths About SFC

  • We don’t ignore problems
  • We don’t rush past empathy
  • We don’t leave families to figure things out alone
  • Myth 1: “Do we ignore problems and move straight to solutions?”
    • No. We make space to hear challenges and respond with empathy.
    • We do not need to fully analyze every aspect of the problem before building a preferred future.
  • Myth 2: “Do we simply emphasize strengths and ask families to fix things alone?”
    • No. Families come for support.
    • We bring knowledge, skills, and structure while making space for family expertise and priorities.

The Miracle / Tomorrow Question (Low Drama)

  • Invites a preferred future without requiring full problem analysis
  • Uses imagination to create direction, not fantasy
  • Focuses on observable, everyday differences
  • Works best when there is enough time to explore follow-up questions
  • In classic SFC, this is called the Miracle Question (drama is optional).
  • Optional long-form script:
    • “So imagine you’ve told me about the problem and it is clear. I have a magic wand, but it only works overnight while you sleep. Tomorrow morning, what would be the first sign that tells you the magic worked?”
  • Softer version (Tomorrow Question):
    • “Suppose tomorrow is just a little better. What would you notice first?”
  • The goal is not the miracle itself; it clarifies what matters, what fits, and what is already partly happening.

Using the Miracle / Tomorrow Question Well

  • Keep the image concrete (what people would see, hear, or do differently)
  • Stay anchored in the client’s real context and constraints
  • Translate images into small differences
  • Use follow-ups: scaling, coping, exceptions, relational noticing
  • This question opens a door; it does not walk through it for them.

Using the Miracle / Tomorrow Question - Follow-Up

The real work happens in the follow-ups:

  • “What tells you the magic worked?”
  • “Who would notice first?”
  • “On a scale of 0–10, how much of that is already happening?”
  • Go circular (ask client to be in someone else’s shoes to answer the question):
    • What do you think your mom might answer in your place?
    • How might that be similar or different from your response?
  • Aim to understand the preferred future in concrete detail.
  • Paint the destination, not the full path to get there.
  • This relieves pressure to know exactly what to do in the moment.

Miracle / Tomorrow Question - Example (1/3)

Coach: Imagine you go to bed tonight and while you’re sleeping, a miracle happens. When you wake up, the challenges you’re facing as a caregiver are resolved. What’s the first sign you’ll notice that tells you the miracle occurred?
Caregiver: I guess, I’d wake up feeling rested for once. I’d be able to enjoy my morning coffee in peace, without feeling rushed or worried.

Miracle / Tomorrow Question - Example (2/3)

Coach: That sounds peaceful. What else would be different in your day?
Caregiver: I’d have time to take care of myself, maybe exercise or read. And I’d feel more patient and present with my loved one.

Miracle / Tomorrow Question - Example (3/3)

Coach: How would these changes make you feel about your caregiving role?
Caregiver: More balanced and less stressed, definitely. I’d feel like I’m not just surviving, but actually living.
Coach: Let’s explore how we can move towards that feeling. What small steps can you take today that align with this “miracle” scenario?

Responding to “I dunno”

  • Wait. Maybe they need time to process. The better your rapport, the more you can sit in silence.
  • What’s your best guess? Relieves pressure of being “right.”
  • Go relational. Ask client to be in someone else’s shoes to answer the question: what do you think your mom might answer in your place? How might that be similar or different from your response?
  • Use normalizing language when clients get stuck.
  • Example: “Other families with similar concerns sometimes find this question hard to answer. A best guess is enough to get us started, and we can make sense of it together.”

Compliments as Questions

  • Recognize effort without evaluation or praise
  • Help clients notice skills they are already using
  • Frame strengths as resources, not personality traits
  • Keep the client in the position of meaning-maker
  • In SFC, compliments are not “You’re doing great.”
  • They sound more like:
    • “How did you manage that?”
    • “What does that say about what matters to you?”
  • We are inviting reflection, not delivering a verdict.

What Compliments-as-Questions Do

  • Reduce shame and defensiveness
  • Restore a sense of competence and agency
  • Shift attention from failure to capacity under pressure
  • Support movement toward next steps
  • Stress hides competence; it does not erase it.
  • Our questions help bring that competence back into view.

Practice: Compliments as Questions

Brief scenario:
A caregiver says: “This week was rough, but I still got my son to school three times and managed one calm bedtime.”

  • Write 2-3 compliment-as-questions you could ask next.
  • Aim for questions that highlight effort, strategy, and values (not praise).
  • Share one question with the group.

Noticing Tasks & Small Experiments

  • Build change through attention, not instruction
  • Focus on what is already working or improving
  • Co-design one small experiment between meetings
  • Treat outcomes as information, not success/failure
  • These are not homework assignments in the school sense.
  • They are invitations to notice:
    • “What difference does it make when…?”
    • “What happens if you do a bit more of that?”

Designing Noticing Tasks That Fit Families

  • Keep tasks small, specific, and flexible
  • Fit them into daily life – not extra work
  • Align with the family’s priorities and energy
  • Review with curiosity, not judgment
  • If a task does not “work,” that is still useful information.
  • We are learning together what fits this family, not testing performance.
  • This works best when ideas come from them.
  • When meeting with more than one person, each person is responsible for their own tasks (not someone else’s).

SFC Skills Summary

  • Acknowledgment of struggle: recognize and validate the caregiver’s feelings and challenges.
  • Exceptions: when is the problem not happening? When is it less intense?
  • Scaling questions: use scaling to understand the caregiver’s current state and desired state.
  • Miracle question: there will be some challenges here.
  • Compliments: highlight and praise the caregiver’s efforts and strengths.
  • Coach observation skills: noticing exceptions, changes, and hopes.

Bridge: Skills -> Family Partnering and Joining

  • Skills work best when families feel respected and included.
  • Joining protects alliance under stress.
  • Partnering keeps plans realistic, culturally responsive, and sustainable.

Family, Context, Resilience, and Partnership

  • Caregiver challenges are relational and systemic.
  • Family voice, agency, and participation are core practice needs.
  • Partnership requires both lived and professional expertise.

What Do We Mean by “Family”?

  • Family is defined by the people living it.
  • Family may be biological, chosen, or community-based.
  • Roles and structure vary across culture and life stage.
  • May be more about what family does than membership rules.

What Family Resilience Is

  • To do well in the face of adversity.
  • Resilience is a process, not a trait.
  • Families navigate and negotiate for meaningful supports.
  • Small shifts can restore hope and direction.

Activating Families

  • Voice: families are heard in their own words.
  • Agency: families make meaningful choices.
  • Participation: families shape plans, not just receive them.

Partnership

Section 4B

Joining as Practice

Joining as Practice

  • Joining is an ongoing active intervention, not a warm-up step.
  • SFC assumptions change how we listen and respond.
  • Joining continues through the whole conversation.
  • Improves readiness for change.

SFC Changes How We Join

  • Listen for hopes and priorities
  • Listen for coping and exceptions
  • Listen for readiness language (motivational interviewing)

Summarize, Validate, and Empathize

  • Summarize, validate, and empathize.
  • “What I hear you saying is …”
  • “It makes sense that you’re experiencing/feeling ____ because ….”
  • “While I haven’t been through exactly what you have, I can imagine that it must feel _____ .”
  • “Did I get that right?”
  • Avoid sharing your own story until there is evidence the family feels fully heard.
  • If you share, keep it brief and clearly relevant.
  • Check understanding often.
  • Invite correction without defensiveness.
  • Co-construct meaning before action planning.

Summarize, Validate, and Empathize (1/4)

Client: I am exhausted. Every day feels like too much, and I feel like if I drop one thing, everything will fall apart.
Coach: You’re really at your wits end. It feels like a breaking point for you because you have so much on your plate and that you need to manage everything on your own.

Summarize, Validate, and Empathize (2/4)

Client: I’ve been trying to hold everything together for months, and I honestly do not know how much longer I can keep this up.
Coach: It makes sense that you’re feeling burnt out when you’ve been doing it all on your own for so long and you don’t see any other way to keep afloat.

Summarize, Validate, and Empathize (3/4)

Client: I keep looking for another option, but every path feels blocked and it is hard not to feel defeated.
Coach: I’ve never had that same pile-up of stressors to deal with, but I can imagine that it must be discouraging when you’re faced with all of this pressure and you can’t really see any alternative.

Summarize, Validate, and Empathize (4/4)

Coach: Did I get that right?

Validate Without Reinforcing Stuck Stories

  • Validate emotion and effort directly.
  • Avoid deepening helplessness narratives.
  • Pivot gently toward preferred direction.

Managing Our Own Emotional Responses

  • Notice urgency, rescuing, frustration.
  • Pause before giving advice.
  • Return to curiosity and collaboration.
  • Share your story when it is a scar, not a scab.

Reflective Summaries : How We Can Check-In

  • “What I am hearing is…”
  • “The part that matters most to you is…”
  • “Did I get that right?”

Validation + Curiosity : Evidence of Listening

  • “It makes sense this feels exhausting.”
  • “How have you managed on the hardest days?”
  • “What might feel slightly more manageable this week?”
