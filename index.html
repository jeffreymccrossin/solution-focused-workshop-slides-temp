Background: What Is Solution-Focused Coaching?

Solution-Focused Coaching is a collaborative approach that:

Uses goal-oriented, collaborative conversations.

Focuses on direction, strengths, and workable next steps.

Fits caregiver and family support contexts.

Works within real-world constraints (time and complexity).

Background: Brief Origins

Brief origins and evolution of SFC:

Emerged from brief, practice-based therapy traditions.

Controversial: long treatment history is not required.

Adapted across health, education, social, and community settings.

Core thread: useful conversations that move people forward.

Case Example Revisited

What we know

  • Mother of a 9-year-old with developmental needs calls hotline.
  • She feels done and doubts support will help.
  • Prior services felt unhelpful.
  • Flat tone, brief answers, declines group support.
  • Night shift + no backup childcare.

Team reaction

“She sounds unmotivated and resistant. Send resources by email and close unless she calls back.”

Pause question

How can we apply SFC assumptions?

  • How did your thinking change?
  • Clients have resources: Treat “flat tone/brief answers” as fatigue and protection, not resistance.
  • Collaboration over expert-driven fixing: Ask what she wants from this contact before offering options.
  • Small changes matter: Co-create one realistic next step that fits night-shift/no-childcare constraints.
  • Exceptions exist: Explore when caregiving felt even slightly more manageable and what was different.
  • Future focus: Define a near-future sign of progress (next 48 hours or this week).
  • Do more of what works: Identify any prior support element that helped, even briefly, and adapt it.
  • Language shapes change: Replace “unmotivated/resistant” with context-based descriptions and strengths.
  • Pacing and fit: Keep contact brief, concrete, and choice-based; do not overload with resources.
  • Scenario reminder: mother calls caregiver support hotline about her 9-year-old son with developmental needs.

Case Example Revisited: SFC Reframe

1. Re-interpret

  • Not “resistant.”
  • Likely exhausted, discouraged, and protecting energy.

2. First response

  • Validate effort and strain.
  • Ask what would be useful from this call.
  • Offer one choice-sized next step.

3. SFC next step

  • “What would feel 10% more manageable this week?”
  • “When is this a little less intense?”
  • “What already helps, even briefly?”
  • Debrief prompts:
  • “What would our plan be right now?”
  • “What assumptions are we making about motivation?”
  • “What alternative interpretation and next step would improve engagement?”

Solution Talk: What Works?

Solution talk & future focus People can get stuck trying to understand how the problem developed.

Problem Talk Create enough space for people to feel heard before shifting the conversation.

What’s Better? Track small improvements and build from progress already happening.

  • Explore past attempts (failed and successful) to solve the problem.
  • What worked before, even just a little?
  • Exceptions to the problem: when is the problem absent, or less intense?
  • What works for some family members in certain situations?
  • This approach contrasts common red-flag words from clients: everyone, always, never.

Coping Questions

Coping How do they manage?

  • Used when things feel stuck, overwhelming, or crisis-oriented
  • Acknowledge difficulty without reinforcing the problem story
  • Highlight how people are already managing to get through
  • Restore a sense of competence, dignity, and agency
  • Create enough stability to move toward exceptions and next steps
  • Coping questions are not about minimizing suffering.
  • They recognize what it takes to keep going when things are hard.
  • They pause the problem spiral and help clients notice resilience already present, even when it does not feel like “strength.”

Coping Questions - Example (1/3)

Coach: I understand caregiving can be incredibly challenging. When it feels overwhelming, how do you usually cope?
Caregiver: Sometimes I take a short walk, or I call a friend. It doesn’t always help, but it gives me a little break.

Coping Questions - Example (2/3)

Coach: Those sound like valuable strategies. How effective are they in reducing your stress?
Caregiver: They help a bit, but I often feel guilty for taking time for myself.
Coach: What if you could take these breaks without feeling guilty? What might that look like?

Coping Questions - Example (3/3)

Caregiver: I guess reminding myself that taking care of my needs helps me be a better caregiver.
Coach: That is a great perspective. How might you incorporate these breaks more regularly so they become a guilt-free part of caregiving?

Scaling Questions

Scaling questions Take stock of where clients have been, where they are now, and what they expect next.

  • Creates a shared, concrete snapshot of where things are now.
  • Good for testing your understanding.
  • Surfaces existing progress and resources (why not lower?).
  • Supports collaborative goal setting by identifying the next small gain (one point higher).

Scaling Questions - Example (A 1/3)

Coach: On a scale of 1-10, where 1 is the worst and 10 is much easier to manage, where are you today?
Client: Maybe a 5.5.
Coach (Option A): Why 5.5 and not 5?
Coach (Option B): Why 5.5 and not 0?

Scaling Questions - Example (A 2/3)

Coach (option A): What would be happening in your life when you notice you are at a solid 6?
Coach (option B): What will you notice when you find yourself at a 10?

Scaling Questions - Example (A 3/3)

Coach (option C): At what number would you be satisfied? What would be happening then?
Coach (relational): What would others notice about you?
Coach (relational): What number might your partner/mother/child guess you are at today?

Scaling Questions - Example (B 1/2)

Coach: So, you’d be satisfied with an 8. What would be happening then?
Client: My husband would be drinking less.

Scaling Questions - Example (B 2/2)

Coach: Right, if he were drinking less you’d notice a big difference. What difference would that make to you if he were to be sober?

Dispelling Common Myths About SFC

  • We don’t ignore problems
  • We don’t rush past empathy
  • We don’t leave families to figure things out alone
  • Myth 1: “Do we ignore problems and move straight to solutions?”
    • No. We make space to hear challenges and respond with empathy.
    • We do not need to fully analyze every aspect of the problem before building a preferred future.
  • Myth 2: “Do we simply emphasize strengths and ask families to fix things alone?”
    • No. Families come for support.
    • We bring knowledge, skills, and structure while making space for family expertise and priorities.

Miracle / Tomorrow Question

Miracle/Tomorrow (1-3)

1

Low drama framing

2

Use it well

3

Follow-up prompts

  • Invites a preferred future without requiring full problem analysis
  • Uses imagination to create direction, not fantasy
  • Focuses on observable, everyday differences
  • Works best when there is enough time to explore follow-up questions
  • In classic SFC, this is called the Miracle Question (drama is optional).
  • Optional long-form script:
    • “So imagine you’ve told me about the problem and it is clear. I have a magic wand, but it only works overnight while you sleep. Tomorrow morning, what would be the first sign that tells you the magic worked?”
  • Softer version (Tomorrow Question):
    • “Suppose tomorrow is just a little better. What would you notice first?”
  • The goal is not the miracle itself; it clarifies what matters, what fits, and what is already partly happening.

Miracle / Tomorrow Question

Miracle/Tomorrow (1-3)

1

Low drama framing

2

Use it well

3

Follow-up prompts

The real work happens in the follow-ups:

  • “What tells you the magic worked?”
  • “Who would notice first?”
  • “On a scale of 0–10, how much of that is already happening?”
  • Go circular:
    • What do you think your mom might answer in your place?
    • How might that be similar or different from your response?
  • Aim to understand the preferred future in concrete detail.
  • Paint the destination, not the full path to get there.
  • This relieves pressure to know exactly what to do in the moment.

Miracle / Tomorrow Question - Example (1/3)

Coach: Imagine you go to bed tonight and while you’re sleeping, a miracle happens. When you wake up, the challenges you’re facing as a caregiver are resolved. What’s the first sign you’ll notice that tells you the miracle occurred?

Miracle / Tomorrow Question - Example (2/3)

Miracle / Tomorrow Question - Example (3/3)

Coach: How would these changes make you feel about your caregiving role?
Caregiver: More balanced and less stressed, definitely. I’d feel like I’m not just surviving, but actually living.
Coach: Let’s explore how we can move towards that feeling. What small steps can you take today that align with this “miracle” scenario?

Responding to “I dunno”

  • Wait. Maybe they need time to process. The better your rapport, the more you can sit in silence.
  • What’s your best guess? Relieves pressure of being “right.”
  • Go relational. Ask client to be in someone else’s shoes to answer the question: what do you think your mom might answer in your place? How might that be similar or different from your response?
  • Use normalizing language when clients get stuck.
  • Example: “Other families with similar concerns sometimes find this question hard to answer. A best guess is enough to get us started, and we can make sense of it together.”

1

Small experiments
